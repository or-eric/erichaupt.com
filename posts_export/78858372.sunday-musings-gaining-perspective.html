I’m going to start this one off with story time. Earlier this week I had a new college graduate enter my office to start an internship while waiting for her next bout of schooling. We started with the expected banal introductions:

“How was school? What’s next? What are you doing while you’re here?”

I started to think about my time as a fresh 2nd Lieutenant and about what I wished I had seniors do.

SO, we discussed her mentors in school, while she’s here, what her professional development plan is. Nothing; no plan to help her outside of “read doctrine”.

I’m baffled; in today’s age of endless information at our fingertips, I didn’t immediately have a plan to help her. Shame on me. I’ve fixed the issue.

To keep this short, I’m glad this happened. I took a look at my repository of knowledge and I’m extremely lacking in diverse optics. Leading is not only about disappointing people at a rate that they can accept, but it also about being the leader that is needed, not the one you think you should be. You have to find ways to connect with your audience, it is on you to connect to them, not them to connect to you.

What I’m Reading This Week

The 4-Hour Workweek, by Tim Ferriss

I’ve only just started Tim’s book. I think that makes me only 15 years late to the party, but hey, I’m here now and I brought high quality snacks! My thin slice of the book (that’s me looking at the table of contents, and a quick skim of the first page or two in each chapter) tells me he’s going to resonate with the way I think.

My summary of the concept is, find what you love and do that, question traditional models, don’t waste your life for the big payoff. Be effective at what you do that’s important, be efficient at the unimportant things that still need to get done. I believe that’s also similar to the Pareto’s Law (80/20 rule)? Focus on the 20% of things that bring you closer to your goals, be that making more money, or spend your time with friends and family. A lack of time for the things you need to get done means you’re likely spending time on the wrong things. Manage time effectively and eliminate time wasters.

Tim wrote this going into a recession; apropos considering where we are headed today. I’ll circle back in a couple weeks to this and see how far off I am (probably) and proffer a recommendation.

-Booker T. Washington

I like this one. I think it goes nicely with defining courage not as doing something without fear, but by doing so despite fear. Our struggles are all a part of the crucible of life, meant to mold and shape us, temper our nature and harden our resolve. How boring life would be if there were never a challenge.
